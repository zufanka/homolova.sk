 Make internet search your own!

Internet search sucks. It’s not only my personal opinion, as a quick internet search confirms:

Researchers from German universities came to the same conclusion: “We can conclude that higher-ranked pages are on average more optimized, more monetized with affiliate marketing, and they show signs of lower text quality”, reads their recent paper.

The search is also probably going to get worse. With the dawn of generative AI, the internet will never be the same as when it was populated just by us humans. And as much as I love robots, they also produce text that contradicts itself in two consecutive sentences, or in a much worse case it helps you poison yourself with mushrooms.

Still, the deepest wish of every website is to be found. And internet search is the most important way we navigate the vast lands of the internet. A search engine is like a map, albeit one that always shows just a select piece of the landscape. Search is so important that it even shapes the internet (and The Verge wrote a beautiful article about that).

So how to search in between all the advertisement farms and AI generated slob? Can we look beyond the big news websites for interesting articles to read? The good news is: you don’t have to keep on Googling, there is much more out there!

Take my hand into the expanding lands of internet search.

On a daily basis, I use a couple of different search engines. All of them have a different approach to indexing the web and this helps me to think better about what results I actually want to achieve by my searches.

Internet anno 2024 is quite a different animal than it was 10 years ago. Now websites with extensive SEO strategies and big platforms (BBC, Medium, Reddit) are deemed more relevant for search engines like Google or Bing. I therefore like to make an effort to look further.

Disclaimer: The search engines below are just my personal blend to encourage and inspire: Make search your own!

Ecosia - the default 

Ecosia is my default search engine. I use it when I know what I am searching for: company names, people, wikipedia articles, documentation. Under the hood it uses two of the big boys : Google and Microsoft Bing. The search is therefore still not better than Google, but their business model I like. Ecosia is a not-for-profit company that puts their revenues into planting trees.

Next to search, they also offer an AI chat feature, based on GPT-3. This one I use for translations and when I need definitions of some term or phenomenon. I also use it for basic recipes, and tweaks for recipes. But for recipes in general, I actually like to use another search engine.

Marginalia - the warm

Marginalia is what I like to see as a serendipity search engine: you don’t really know what you get. The index is built by a Swedish developer Viktor Löfgren on mostly grant money (he also has a patreon) and focuses on “small obscure websites and non-commercial content”, as the author writes. 

What I love about Marginalia is that when I searched for a recipe for a crumble, instead of one of the sterile over-optimized recipe pages, I discovered the Kitchen Projects substack, where somebody called Nicola Lamb documents their experiments in crumble baking. Next to the recipe, she also shares explanations of how baking with fruit works. If this doesn’t give you warm fuzzy feelings for the internet, I don’t know what does.

I use Marginalia if I am interested in anything related to craft (cooking, basket weaving) or if I feel like exploring something online, but don’t feel like hanging out on social media. Their rainbow ‘random’ button is perfect for that.

Google - the necessary evil

Google is not completely out of my system, but I currently use it only when I can not find things via Ecosia. Something where Google shines is finding out where to buy things online, so I do my infrequent online shopping ventures on Google too.

Exa - the new shiny toy

Exa is an LLM based search engine that took on quite the ambitious goal to fix the internet search. I discovered it only recently, but I like it a lot and use it regularly to discover material around a certain topic for my work related searches. 

Where Exa also provides something unique is that it lets you chat with the individual search results pages. It also formulates new questions for you to search for based on the current search. One to keep an eye on!

Kagi - the premium

Kagi is the only search engine in the list that you need to pay for if you want to do more than 100 searches a month. I therefore keep it as the last option. I do have to admit the search results are very relevant and it helps me find things that I could not find via any of the other search engines.

Are there search engines you use and love? I’m curious to hear!
